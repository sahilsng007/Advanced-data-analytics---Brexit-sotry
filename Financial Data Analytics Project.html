1 Introduction

1.1 Research Questions and Project Overview

This report investigates the volatility and market linkages of the USD/GBP exchange rate over the last decade. It is structured around three interconnected questions that progressively build a layered understanding of exchange rate behavior and market spillovers:

1. Exploratory Analysis: How has the volatility of the USD/GBP exchange rate changed over time?

This question identifies a) patterns of fluctuation, b) clusters of high volatility, and c) the empirical distribution of returns.

2. Time Series Modelling: What is the most suitable GARCH model to quantify and forecast volatility in the USD/GBP exchange rate?

By capturing time-varying volatility, we evaluate the performance of sGARCH and eGARCH models across stress periods.

3. Advanced Analysis: Do GBP/USD exchange rate shocks transmit to the FTSE 100 index?

Using a Vector Auto-regression (VAR) framework, we test for return spillovers and causal dynamics between currency and equity markets.

These questions form a logical progression, from uncovering volatility patterns, to modelling their dynamics, to exploring their wider financial implications.

1.2 Background

Exchange rates serve as barometers of economic health and investor sentiment. For the UK, key geopolitical events like the Brexit referendum (2016) and COVID-19 crisis (2020) caused sharp drops in GBP/USD, triggering systemic volatility. Brexit saw the pound’s largest single-day drop in over 30 years, while COVID-19 led to levels not seen since the mid-1980s. These periods provide rich ground for volatility analysis and raise important questions about inter-market risk transmission.

1.3 Research Objectives

This project aims to:

  • Identify and visualise volatility clusters in the GBP/USD series.

  • Quantify volatility persistence using GARCH models.

  • Test whether exchange rate shocks spill over to the FTSE 100 using VAR.

Together, these goals guide a comprehensive understanding of volatility formation, forecasting, and contagion risk.

1.4 Literature Review

Volatility clustering and heavy tails are well-documented in financial time series (Cont, 2001; Andersen et al., 2001). GARCH models (Bollerslev, 1986) are widely used to model these dynamics, particularly during shocks (Darvas & Schepp, 2024). VAR models (Sims, 1980) allow us to investigate market interactions and shock transmission, with Forbes and Rigobon (2002) and Narayan (2021) showing contagion effects during crises like COVID-19.

2 Data and Methods

2.1 Data Description

This study utilizes two key financial time series datasets sourced from the tsfe R package. The primary dataset is the USD/GBP exchange rate, originally spanning from January 2010 to mid-January 2020 and sourced from the Bank of England. To capture the heightened market uncertainty during the early COVID-19 period, I manually extended the series by adding 50 daily observations from 17 January to 31 March 2020. This extension was crucial to fully represent the steep depreciation of the British pound on 18 March 2020 during the global lockdown announcement.

The exchange rate data was cleaned and transformed into an xts time series object to facilitate use in modeling packages like rugarch and forecast. From this cleaned dataset, log returns were computed as the first difference of the log of exchange rate levels, multiplied by 100 to yield percentage returns. This transformation helps stabilize the variance and is widely adopted in financial econometrics.

To support inter-market analysis in the final stage of the project, I also included the FTSE All-Share Price Index, also sourced from tsfe::indices. After aligning the date ranges and cleaning for missing values, I calculated log returns for the FTSE index in the same manner. The final dataset includes synchronized daily log returns for both the GBP/USD exchange rate and the FTSE index, allowing for bivariate analysis using the Vector Auto-regressive (VAR) model.

2.2 Data Preparation

The first step in preparation involved converting the USD/GBP series into a tidy tibble structure and renaming variables for clarity. As the original dataset ended before the pandemic-induced volatility, I manually appended 50 new rows via tribble() to include data through the end of March 2020. This ensured that the dataset captured one of the most volatile episodes in recent currency history.

After merging the new and original series, duplicates were removed and the full dataset was ordered by date. Log returns were then calculated using the formula:

rt=ln⁡(PtPt−1)×100,where Pt is the price on day t

To visualise long-term currency dynamics, I computed and plotted average annual exchange rates between 2010 and 2020. This revealed a steady depreciation trend, with a sharp structural break occurring in 2016—coinciding with the Brexit referendum—and another decline in March 2020 during the COVID-19 crisis.

For the advanced analysis in Section 3, I prepared a merged dataset combining the GBP/USD and FTSE series. After aligning dates and dropping missing values, log returns were calculated for both series. These return series served as the basis for VAR estimation and subsequent shock transmission analysis.

Collectively, the data preparation ensured:

  • Full coverage of crisis periods (Brexit, COVID-19)
  • Clean and stationary return series for modeling
  • Bivariate alignment for VAR-based spillover testing

2.3 Methodology

2.3.1 Exploratory Analysis Methods

The exploratory analysis focused on understanding the statistical properties of the GBP/USD exchange rate returns. A histogram and density plot of the log returns were used to assess normality and identify fat tails. To further explore distributional properties, a QQ plot was generated, which revealed heavy-tailed behaviour compared to the Gaussian benchmark.

Time series plots of the log returns highlighted visually distinct periods of high volatility—most notably in 2016 and 2020. To investigate volatility clustering, we computed and visualised the autocorrelation functions (ACF) of both raw returns and squared returns. While the raw return series showed little autocorrelation, the squared returns exhibited significant persistence, consistent with the presence of ARCH effects.

These observations validated the need for formal volatility modeling via GARCH-family models.

2.3.2 Time Series Modeling Methods

To model and forecast time-varying volatility, I estimated a series of GARCH-family models using the rugarch package. The mean equation was specified as ARMA(1,0), chosen based on AIC minimization and the autocorrelation structure of the returns. I estimated three competing models: sGARCH(1,1), EGARCH(1,1), and GJR-GARCH(1,1).

Model selection was based on log-likelihood, Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), and Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). Residual diagnostics included:

  • Ljung-Box tests for serial correlation in residuals
  • ARCH LM tests for remaining ARCH effects
  • Sign bias tests for asymmetry in volatility

Out-of-sample forecasts were performed using a rolling 100-day window to simulate real-world prediction. Forecast plots included conditional volatility bands (±2σ), offering intuitive measures of expected uncertainty.

2.3.3 Advanced Analysis Methods

For Section 3, I used a Vector Autoregressive (VAR) framework to investigate spillover effects from currency returns to the domestic stock market. After confirming stationarity using the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test, I determined the optimal lag length using AIC and Schwarz Criterion (SC).

The VAR model enabled:

  • Granger causality testing to assess predictive relationships
  • Impulse response functions (IRF) to visualise the effect of GBP shocks on FTSE returns
  • Forecast error variance decomposition (FEVD) to determine the relative contribution of each market to future uncertainty

This multivariate approach provided insights into how financial shocks propagate across interconnected markets, particularly during high-stress events like Brexit and the COVID-19 pandemic.

Before fitting any time series model, we need to ensure the data is stationary. The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test confirms that the USD/GBP return series is stationary (Dickey-Fuller = –14.33, p < 0.01), meaning its statistical properties remain stable over time. This clears the way for models like ARIMA and GARCH.

We began by estimating a standard GARCH(1,1) with AR(1) mean. The results were no surprise: both short-run shocks (α₁ = 0.084) and long-run persistence (β₁ = 0.903) were significant, and ω = 0.00547 is capturing the steady-state volatility. Since α + β < 1, the process is stable but very persistent—shocks like Brexit or COVID-19 don’t wear off quickly.

Model diagnostics preferred a good fit. Ljung-Box tests confirmed there was no residual autocorrelation, and the Sign Bias test indicated there was no asymmetry, so a symmetric GARCH specification was required. There was no residual ARCH effect either.

Practically, this confirms that while GBP/USD returns are hard to predict directionally, their volatility is anything but random. This underlines the need for risk management instruments responding to volatility instead of returns. These findings validate our modeling approach and open the door to exploring more flexible GARCH models and spillover dynamics in the following sections.

3.4 Discussion

3.4.1 Integration of Findings

GBP/USD stress behavior and whether it spillovers on UK equities were differentiated in this project. Rolling standard deviations and ACF plots exploratory techniques picked up notable volatility clustering around Brexit and the COVID-19 crash—two of the most confounding events in recent financial history.

Time series analysis confirmed that volatility was not just noise. EGARCH(1,1) with Student-t distribution totally dominated, capturing the persistence and asymmetry of volatility in response to shocks—volatility responded more to bad news than to good. This was consistent with true behavior during economic and political crises.

The VAR model extended this, examining the influence of currency market shocks on equity markets. Though Granger causality was non-existent, the instantaneous spillovers were massive—i.e., both GBP and FTSE 100 returns react in real time to other news, such as announcements by central banks or global risk occurrences.

Every test naturally evolved from the last one. Collectively, they establish GBP/USD volatility to be long-run stable, asymmetric, and part of a general real-time system of market reactions.

3.4.2 Practical Implications

The findings are pertinent to policymakers and risk managers. Volatility doesn’t drop off suddenly—models need to keep this persistence or else they end up underpriced risk. The EGARCH model’s responsiveness to asymmetry is invaluable in stress tests and crisis management.

The VAR outputs capture the way markets coordinate their response to shared shocks, although they do not forecast one another. To policymakers, that means looking to the broader world—primarily FX markets—for direction. To investors, it provides room for policy responses to converge in periods of global unease.

3.4.3 Limitations

This study utilized intraday returns, which are strong but brief market movements. Intraday returns could be more precise in the type of volatility at announcements or flash crashes.

Also, as handy as the bivariate VAR setup was, it did not encompass other such variables such as interest rates, inflation, or world indices, which could conceivably affect both markets. And, as with all models, outcomes are a matter of design decisions—lag length, error distributions, and sample horizons all come into play.

3.4.4 Future Research

Future research could explore DCC-GARCH models and identify how market correlations change over time. Machine learning models like LSTMs could even be able to identify non-linearities and structural breaks that linear models cannot.

Including macro variables in the VAR model would tell us more about what at the same time induces market behavior, especially during crisis periods.

This research chronicled the GBP/USD volatility story through two major stress events—COVID-19 and Brexit—and monitored its UK equity market correlations. We found compelling evidence of clustering, asymmetry, and real-time cross-market reactions. Our findings vindicate why volatility can never be lonely—and why adaptive, multi-layer models are needed to untangle markets today.

## Methodology

### Exploratory Analysis Methods

The exploratory analysis focused on understanding the statistical properties of the GBP/USD exchange rate returns. A histogram and density plot of the log returns were used to assess normality and identify fat tails. To further explore distributional properties, a QQ plot was generated, which revealed heavy-tailed behaviour compared to the Gaussian benchmark.

Time series plots of the log returns highlighted visually distinct periods of high volatility—most notably in 2016 and 2020. To investigate volatility clustering, we computed and visualised the autocorrelation functions (ACF) of both raw returns and squared returns. While the raw return series showed little autocorrelation, the squared returns exhibited significant persistence, consistent with the presence of ARCH effects.

These observations validated the need for formal volatility modeling via GARCH-family models.

### Time Series Modeling Methods

To model and forecast time-varying volatility, I estimated a series of GARCH-family models using the `rugarch` package. The mean equation was specified as ARMA(1,0), chosen based on AIC minimization and the autocorrelation structure of the returns. I estimated three competing models: sGARCH(1,1), EGARCH(1,1), and GJR-GARCH(1,1).

Model selection was based on log-likelihood, Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), and Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). Residual diagnostics included:

-   Ljung-Box tests for serial correlation in residuals
-   ARCH LM tests for remaining ARCH effects
-   Sign bias tests for asymmetry in volatility

Out-of-sample forecasts were performed using a rolling 100-day window to simulate real-world prediction. Forecast plots included conditional volatility bands (±2σ), offering intuitive measures of expected uncertainty.

### Advanced Analysis Methods

For Section 3, I used a Vector Autoregressive (VAR) framework to investigate spillover effects from currency returns to the domestic stock market. After confirming stationarity using the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test, I determined the optimal lag length using AIC and Schwarz Criterion (SC).

The VAR model enabled:

-   **Granger causality testing** to assess predictive relationships

VAR Model Results
To explore the interaction between currency and equity markets, we fitted a Vector Autoregression (VAR) model using the two stationary return series. Although AIC and FPE suggested a lag of 4, we opted for a more stable VAR(2) specification.

The results show that GBP returns exhibit short-term autocorrelation—lag 2 was significant (p = 0.002). However, FTSE returns showed only weak autocorrelation at lag 1 (p = 0.041), and there was no statistically significant spillover from GBP to FTSE or vice versa.

This was confirmed by the forecast error variance decomposition (FEVD), which showed that both GBP and FTSE returns are mostly driven by their own past shocks over a 10-day horizon. The impulse response plots also showed minimal cross-market influence.

In summary
GBP returns: Significant own-lag effect at lag 2 (p = 0.002), indicating short-term persistence.

FTSE returns: Mild autocorrelation at lag 1 (p = 0.042), no impact from GBP.

Spillovers: No strong evidence of predictive influence between markets.

These findings suggest that, during stress periods like Brexit and COVID-19, GBP volatility did not meaningfully spill into UK equities—supporting our hypothesis that the effects were event-specific and short-lived across asset classes.

The impulse response function (IRF) defines that a GBP return shock is correlated with a highly temporary and minute rise in FTSE returns, peaking after two days and then falling off quickly. This confirms weak and transitory spillover from the currency to equity markets.

In order to test for predictability, we carried out a Granger causality test. This was not statistically significant (F = 0.695, p = 0.498), suggesting that GBP returns do not Granger-cause FTSE returns. The instantaneous causality test was extremely highly significant (Chi-squared = 43.7, p < 0.001), suggesting the two markets are moving together to shared shocks—but not predictively.

In general, contemporaneous relation is high but future influence is low. This justifies our decision to simultaneously simulate the two markets and also guarantees that despite shocks hitting both ends, they are most likely going to do so at the same time, not successively.

## Discussion

### Integration of Findings

GBP/USD stress behavior and whether it spillovers on UK equities were differentiated in this project. Rolling standard deviations and ACF plots exploratory techniques picked up notable volatility clustering around Brexit and the COVID-19 crash—two of the most confounding events in recent financial history.

Time series analysis confirmed that volatility was not just noise. EGARCH(1,1) with Student-t distribution totally dominated, capturing the persistence and asymmetry of volatility in response to shocks—volatility responded more to bad news than to good. This was consistent with true behavior during economic and political crises.

The VAR model extended this, examining the influence of currency market shocks on equity markets. Though Granger causality was non-existent, the instantaneous spillovers were massive—i.e., both GBP and FTSE 100 returns react in real time to other news, such as announcements by central banks or global risk occurrences.

Every test naturally evolved from the last one. Collectively, they establish GBP/USD volatility to be long-run stable, asymmetric, and part of a general real-time system of market reactions.

### Practical Implications

The findings are pertinent to policymakers and risk managers. Volatility doesn't drop off suddenly—models need to keep this persistence or else they end up underpriced risk. The EGARCH model's responsiveness to asymmetry is invaluable in stress tests and crisis management.

The VAR outputs capture the way markets coordinate their response to shared shocks, although they do not forecast one another. To policymakers, that means looking to the broader world—primarily FX markets—for direction. To investors, it provides room for policy responses to converge in periods of global unease.

### Limitations

This study utilized intraday returns, which are strong but brief market movements. Intraday returns could be more precise in the type of volatility at announcements or flash crashes.

Also, as handy as the bivariate VAR setup was, it did not encompass other such variables such as interest rates, inflation, or world indices, which could conceivably affect both markets. And, as with all models, outcomes are a matter of design decisions—lag length, error distributions, and sample horizons all come into play.

### Future Research

Future research could explore DCC-GARCH models and identify how market correlations change over time. Machine learning models like LSTMs could even be able to identify non-linearities and structural breaks that linear models cannot.

Including macro variables in the VAR model would tell us more about what at the same time induces market behavior, especially during crisis periods.

### Concluding Remarks

This research chronicled the GBP/USD volatility story through two major stress events—COVID-19 and Brexit—and monitored its UK equity market correlations. We found compelling evidence of clustering, asymmetry, and real-time cross-market reactions. Our findings vindicate why volatility can never be lonely—and why adaptive, multi-layer models are needed to untangle markets today.
